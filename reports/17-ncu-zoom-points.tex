\begin{abstract}
Report~\texttt{16} measured, on synthetic specs, that the coherent regions GPU
affinity routes to the device run \emph{divergence-free} and \emph{FP64-bound}.
This report verifies that on the \textbf{four \texttt{13-zoom-points.tex}
geometries} (outside, inside, Misiurewicz, seahorse) --- the real workload
shapes --- and adds \textbf{Misiurewicz}, which report~16 never covered. Same
\texttt{ncu} methodology: GPU-only capture so the device sees every region type;
the same four report-13 targets, with frame count reduced $100\to24$ (warp and
FP64 metrics are \emph{rates}, independent of frame count). The result reproduces
exactly: leaves of \textbf{480$\times$270 and larger run at 99.7--100\,\% warp
execution efficiency in every regime} (0\,\% below 70\,\%), while divergence
(down to \textbf{38\,\%}) is \textbf{entirely confined to the 240$\times$135
pixel-floor leaves} (mean $\sim$89\,\%, 8--14\,\% below 70\,\%). The inside point
is FP64-bound at \textbf{85.6\,\% of peak} ($\sim$110 of 129~GFLOPS at 1.34~GHz),
matching report~16 to the decimal. Even Misiurewicz's thin filaments obey the
rule: the filament-crossing floor leaves diverge, the big regions do not.
Divergence is confined to the floor tier by the subdivision rule itself ---
independent of geometry.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Setup}

\paragraph{What is being verified.} \texttt{16-ncu-divergence.tex} established,
from Nsight Compute counters, that (i) the coherent interior/exterior regions run
at exactly 100\,\% warp execution efficiency, (ii) the kernel is FP64-bound at
$\sim$85\,\% of the 1:32 double-precision peak, and (iii) divergence is real but
confined to the pixel-floor boundary leaves. Those measurements used \emph{synthetic}
specs. The open question this report closes: does the result hold on the
\emph{actual} four-regime geometries of \texttt{13-zoom-points.tex}, and on
Misiurewicz, which report~16 did not include?

\paragraph{The four points.} Identical centres and deep rects to report~13:
outside $c\!=\!(1,1)$ (escapes at iter~2); inside $c\!=\!(-0.5,0)$ (period-1
interior, every deep pixel reaches \texttt{maxIter}); Misiurewicz
$c\!=\!(-0.77568377,0.13646737)$ (self-similar spirals in fast-escape exterior);
seahorse $c\!=\!(-0.745428,0.113009)$ (filaments + minibrots, the interior-outlier
regime).

\paragraph{Frame reduction.} The capture renders \textbf{24} frames per spec
instead of report~13's 100. Warp execution efficiency, branch efficiency, and
FP64 pipe utilisation are per-kernel \emph{rates}; they do not depend on how many
frames are rendered. The reduction only makes the \texttt{ncu} kernel-replay
tractable (the heaviest regime, inside, drops from 103~s to 24~s GPU-only). First
and last frames, deep-zoom rects, and \texttt{maxIter}$=$10000 are unchanged.

\paragraph{Method.} GPU-only (\texttt{numThreads}=1) so the single GPU thread
processes every region type --- in production the affinity queue routes the
divergent boundary regions to the CPU pool. Each regime captured twice: \texttt{--set
full} (roofline / occupancy / stalls / FP64, with warp efficiency) on the 40
largest kernels, and a two-metric warp$+$branch pass on 300 kernels for the
size-resolved distribution. Counters are admin-gated; capture ran as root via
\texttt{capture.sh}, parsing is unprivileged. GPC clock 1.34~GHz under load.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{Coherent regions are divergence-free on every report-13 point}

Table~\ref{tab:sol}, Figure~\ref{fig:zoom}: across all four geometries the big
leaves run at \textbf{99.6--100.0\,\% warp execution efficiency and 100.0\,\%
branch efficiency}. Table~\ref{tab:size} sharpens it: the $\ge$480$\times$270
leaves never fall below 70\,\% in any regime (0\,\% of them), with a minimum of
96.9\,\%.

The mechanism is the one report~16 identified, now confirmed on the real
geometries: a region only becomes a large leaf if its nine samples passed the
uniformity test, and passing means the escape iterations are tight --- which is
exactly the condition for low intra-warp spread. The uniformity test doubles as a
coherence filter, so the leaves that actually run as large kernels are coherent by
construction, regardless of whether the target is exterior, interior, a spiral, or
a filament field. The verification holds: report~16's 100\,\% result was not an
artifact of synthetic specs.

\subsection{Divergence is confined to the floor tier --- the same way in four
different geometries}

The most striking result is the \emph{uniformity} of Table~\ref{tab:size} and
Figure~\ref{fig:zoom}. The 240$\times$135 floor leaves diverge in every regime
with a mean of 88.7--91.0\,\%, a minimum of 37.9--47.3\,\%, and 8--14\,\% of them
below 70\,\%. These numbers are nearly identical across four targets that look
nothing alike --- a fast-escape exterior, a solid interior, a Misiurewicz spiral,
and a seahorse filament field.

The reason is that divergence is a property of \emph{subdivision}, not of content.
A region keeps splitting while its sample spread is high; the only way a
high-spread region becomes a leaf is by hitting \texttt{pixelSizeThresh} --- the
floor. So the floor-clamped 240$\times$135 leaves are precisely the regions the
uniformity test \emph{refused} to compute at full size, in every regime. Their
internal escape-time spread is what the warp counters see as wasted lanes. The big
leaves, having passed the test, are coherent everywhere. The implication is that
report~16's ``divergence lives only in the floor leaves'' is not regime-specific:
it is a structural consequence of the split rule, and it generalises.

\paragraph{Misiurewicz behaves like the rest.} The one regime report~16 never
measured is the cleanest test, because a Misiurewicz spiral is mostly fast-escape
exterior threaded with thin high-iteration filaments --- the classic divergence
trap. Yet its big leaves are 99.9\,\% efficient and only its 240$\times$135 floor
leaves diverge (mean 89.0\,\%, min 37.9\,\%), exactly like the others. The
filaments do cause divergence, but only where they are caught in a floor-clamped
leaf; everywhere else the spiral's coherent exterior dominates a large coherent
kernel.

\subsection{The FP64 ceiling reproduces to the decimal}

Table~\ref{tab:sol}: the FP64 pipe runs at 84.9--85.5\,\% of peak in every regime,
and the inside point --- the same geometry as report~13's heaviest run --- is
FP64-bound at \textbf{85.6\,\% of peak}, $\sim$110 of 129~GFLOPS at the measured
1.34~GHz. This is identical to report~16's interior measurement. Occupancy is
91--95\,\%, memory throughput is negligible, and the dominant issue stall is
\texttt{long\_scoreboard} at 94--95\,\% in every regime --- the latency of the
serial $z^2{+}c$ recurrence, uniform across content. The FP64-bound,
recurrence-latency-limited characterisation of the kernel is reproduced
independently on four geometries.

\subsection{Why the aggregate looks calmer than report~16}

A reconciliation, so the two reports are not misread as conflicting. Report~16's
\emph{boundary} regime reported 47\,\% of all kernels below 70\,\% efficiency;
here the worst regime has only 11--14\,\%. The floor leaves themselves diverge
identically (mean $\sim$89\,\%, min $\sim$38\,\% in both reports) --- what differs
is their \emph{fraction} of the kernel population. Report~16's boundary spec was a
shallow zoom engineered to maximise boundary content, so floor leaves dominated.
The report-13 specs are full zooms whose kernels are mostly coherent (the big
leaves plus the 480$\times$270 tier, all 100\,\%), so the floor leaves are a
minority tail. Both agree on the physics of a floor leaf; they differ only in how
boundary-heavy the workload is. The production-relevant number is the one here:
on real geometries, the GPU's kernels are coherent at the median (98.5\,\%) and
the divergent floor leaves are both rare and cheap.

\section{Implications}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The report-16 conclusion generalises --- divergence is a
        non-lever.} On four real geometries plus Misiurewicz, the GPU's coherent
        kernels are 100\,\% efficient and the divergence is quarantined to cheap
        floor leaves that affinity routes to the CPU. There is no warp-divergence
        optimisation to pursue for the GPU path on any of these regimes.
  \item \textbf{The FP64 pipe is the geometry-independent GPU floor.} 85\,\% of a
        1:32 ceiling in every regime, recurrence-latency-limited. The only lever
        is fewer iterations (work-reduction); \texttt{float} is precluded by the
        deep-zoom precision (report~11). Periodicity checking should still land on
        the CPU branch first --- it would convert these 100\,\%-efficient kernels
        into divergent ones (report~16).
  \item \textbf{Floor-leaf divergence is structural, not content-specific.} Since
        it depends only on \texttt{pixelSizeThresh} clamping, a smaller
        \texttt{pixelSizeThresh} would shrink the divergent leaves further, but
        those leaves are already a cheap minority on the CPU --- not worth a sweep.
\end{enumerate}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

Re-running report~16's Nsight Compute measurement on the exact four
\texttt{13-zoom-points.tex} geometries reproduces every finding. The coherent
leaves GPU affinity routes to the device run at \textbf{99.6--100.0\,\% warp
execution efficiency} on all four points; the kernel is \textbf{FP64-bound at
85.6\,\% of the 1:32 peak} ($\sim$110 of 129~GFLOPS), recurrence-latency-limited;
and divergence (to \textbf{38\,\%}) is \textbf{entirely confined to the
240$\times$135 pixel-floor leaves}, near-identically across four geometries that
look nothing alike --- including Misiurewicz, whose filaments diverge only inside
floor-clamped leaves. Divergence is a property of the subdivision rule, not of the
zoom target; it is a non-lever, and the GPU floor is the FP64 iteration count.
The result is verified and generalised.
