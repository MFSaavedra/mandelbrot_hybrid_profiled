\begin{abstract}
Reports 25--26 measured frame distribution on a pair whose remote node is
$4.56\times$ \emph{slower} than the laptop. This report reruns both
protocols on the mirrored pair: \textbf{yeco} (Core Ultra~7 265K, 20
threads, RTX~4090, reached through an SSH jump host) is $4.1\times$
\emph{faster} than the laptop (solo $5.15\pm0.10$ vs.\ $21.02\pm1.94$~s).
The role reversal reproduces every report-25 conclusion from the other
side --- equal shares strand the slow node, block orientation is a crude
70/30 weighting, the weighted ideal is $1.24\times$ over the fast node
alone --- and then exposes something the LAN pair never could:
\textbf{with weights calibrated (1:4), both nodes compute at the
$4.14$~s harmonic ideal, yet the end-to-end wall was $13.11\pm1.81$~s ---
worse than equal shares}. The missing $\sim$8~s is \emph{collection}:
per-file scp round trips ($\sim$68~ms/file at $\sim$25~ms RTT) that
equal-share configs hide behind the stranded node's compute and balanced
configs expose. A controlled transfer A/B on the identical 79-file/36~MB
share measures scp at $6.60$~s vs.\ a single tar stream at $0.96$~s
($6.9\times$, byte-identical). Batching collection into one tar stream per
rank drops weighted static to $\mathbf{6.45\pm0.26}$~s ($-51\%$, $\sigma$
$\times7$ tighter) --- parity with yeco-solo-plus-fetch, the
capacity-not-latency ceiling realized for the third time. Dynamic
stealing self-schedules on \emph{lane time} (compute + shipping): under
scp the \emph{slow} node steals 22 frames from the fast one and lands at
the lane-equalizing optimum; after batching, right weights make stealing a
$+21\%$ chunking tax, and wrong weights reveal the $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$
chunk cap throttling the fast node --- the mirror image of the in-flight
defect report~26 fixed.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation: the mirrored pair}
\label{sec:motivation}

The laptop+ivy campaign left two loose ends. First, its conclusions were
all derived on a pair where the \emph{remote} node is the slow one --- ivy
at $0.855$~s/frame vs.\ the laptop hybrid at $0.188$ ---
so ``equal shares strand the wall on the slow node'' had only been
observed with the slow node conveniently at the far end of the SSH link.
Second, both nodes sat on the same LAN, which kept orchestration overhead
at a flat $3.3$--$4.2$~s and made the transport invisible in every
analysis. A new node inverts both conditions at once: \textbf{yeco}
renders the production spec $4.1\times$ faster than the laptop \emph{and}
sits behind a WAN jump host. This report brings the node up (a Makefile
portability fix, \S\ref{sec:setup}), reruns the report-25 equal-share
sweep and the report-26 weighted/dynamic A/B on the new pair, diagnoses
the transport bottleneck the reruns expose, fixes it (one tar stream per
rank, commit \texttt{1b5da5f}), and re-measures.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Nodes.} Node A is the usual laptop (i7-9750H, 6C/12T +
GTX~1660~Ti Max-Q, Arch, g++-15, CUDA~13.2, on AC for every timed run;
args \texttt{12 1 0.1 32768 1 1}). Node B is \textbf{yeco}: Intel
Core Ultra~7 265K (20 threads), RTX~4090 (sm\_89, 24~GB), Ubuntu~24.04,
CUDA~13.3 at \texttt{/usr/local/cuda}, g++~13.3; a desktop, so no
battery gate applies (args \texttt{20 1 0.1 32768 1 1}). Both nodes run
the same code state (\texttt{c5bd3ed}); each builds natively with plain
\texttt{make} after commit \texttt{c8ac920}, which replaced the
Makefile's hard-coded Arch toolchain (\texttt{g++-15},
\texttt{/opt/cuda}) with overridable autodetection: \texttt{CUDAINST ?=}
first existing of \texttt{/opt/cuda}, \texttt{/usr/local/cuda};
\texttt{HOSTCC ?= g++-15} if present else \texttt{g++}; and nvcc invoked
as \texttt{\$(CUDAINST)/bin/nvcc} --- never \texttt{PATH}'s, because
Ubuntu ships a stale apt CUDA~12.0 in \texttt{/usr/bin} whose nvcc
rejects gcc~13.

\paragraph{Network.} yeco is reachable only through a ProxyJump
(\texttt{laptop $\to$ dichato.dcc.uchile.cl $\to$ yeco}), configured as an
\texttt{ssh yeco} alias with connection multiplexing
(\texttt{ControlMaster}/\texttt{ControlPersist}), so no run pays
per-connection handshakes. Measured link properties (warm master): command
round trip $\sim$25~ms; single-file bulk transfer $\sim$30--37~MB/s (20~MB
in $0.66$~s). Both numbers matter in \S\ref{sec:diagnosis}.

\paragraph{Workload and protocol.} Production spec (100 frames,
$1920\times1080$, the $32\times$ cost-trend zoom), \texttt{diffT}=0.1,
\texttt{quiet}=1, \texttt{save}=1, 3 reps per config, same-day in-batch
baselines --- identical to reports 25/26. \texttt{wall\_e2e\_s} times the
whole orchestration (ship spec + ranks + collect); each node's own
\texttt{[total\_elapsed\_s]} is its compute wall (includes local PNG
encode/write, excludes network).

\paragraph{Identity scope.} The benchmarks verify the \emph{union}
(100/100 distinct global frames collected per run). Byte-identity
hybrid-vs-hybrid across this pair is \emph{not expected and not claimed}:
sm\_75+nvcc~13.2 vs.\ sm\_89+nvcc~13.3 can differ in last-ulp FP64
rounding, the same mechanism as the report-20 CPU-vs-CUDA differences.
Report~25's byte-identity guarantee remains scoped to executor-pinned
CPU runs under the plain \texttt{-O2} rule; that check was not re-run on
this pair.

\section{The scripts}
\label{sec:scripts}

Four scripts produced every number in this report. The two orchestrators
are report-25/26 infrastructure, recapped here because the transport fix
lands inside them; the two benchmark drivers are new.

\subsection{\texttt{scripts/dist\_frames.sh} --- static distribution}
\label{sec:distframes}

One invocation renders the whole spec across $N$ nodes: a hosts file
gives one \texttt{host [rdir [args...]]} line per rank (\texttt{":"} =
this machine without SSH), and GNU parallel runs one per-rank job each of
which (1) creates \texttt{rdir/dist\_work/rank$r$} on its host, (2) ships
the spec there, (3) runs the binary with
\texttt{DIST\_NODES/DIST\_RANK/DIST\_BLOCK} (or \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS},
precedence \texttt{DIST\_FRAMES} $>$ \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS} $>$
block-cyclic) selecting that rank's frames inside \texttt{main.cpp}'s
frame loop, and (4) copies the rank's PNGs back to the collection
directory. Because output filenames carry the \emph{global} frame index,
collection is a plain union --- no renumbering, no collisions. Step (4)
is where this report's fix lives (\S\ref{sec:fix}).

\subsection{\texttt{scripts/dist\_dynamic.sh} --- stealing coordinator}

The report-26 dynamic mode: the frame list is shuffled with a seeded
deterministic shuffle and dealt into per-rank bags proportionally to
\texttt{WEIGHTS}; one driver per node then dispatches its bag in guided
chunks --- half the remaining bag, capped at $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$
frames (default $\mathrm{KCAP}=8$), min \texttt{KMIN} --- as repeated
binary invocations with \texttt{DIST\_FRAMES=$\langle$list$\rangle$}. A
driver whose bag empties \emph{steals} half the richest node's
undispatched tail; all bag mutations are serialized under one
\texttt{flock}, and in-flight chunks are already out of the bags, so
every frame renders exactly once. Two properties matter below: each
chunk is a fresh process (so a GPU node pays CUDA-context creation per
chunk), and each chunk's PNGs are copied back \emph{inside the driver
loop, between the chunk and the next \texttt{take\_chunk}} --- so a
node's dispatch cadence includes its shipping.

\subsection{\texttt{bench\_2node\_yeco.sh} and \texttt{bench\_dynamic\_yeco.sh}}

Faithful ports of \texttt{bench\_2node.sh} / \texttt{bench\_dynamic.sh}
(same configs, same CSV schema, same in-batch baseline discipline) with
three deliberate differences. (1)~The remote node is \texttt{yeco} with
args \texttt{20 1 0.1 32768 1 1} --- a \emph{hybrid} node, unlike ivy's
CPU-only build. (2)~The AC gate checks only the laptop: yeco has no
battery to check. (3)~\texttt{yeco\_solo} runs 3 reps instead of ivy's 1
--- at $\sim$5~s it is too cheap not to. The dynamic driver uses weights
\textbf{1:4} (laptop:yeco), the measured throughput ratio
($21.02/5.15 = 4.08$), plus the deliberately wrong \textbf{1:1} row ---
the mirror of report~26's 5:1/1:1 pairing. The full A/B was run twice:
once with the original per-file scp collection
(\texttt{results\_dynamic\_yeco\_scp.csv}, preserved per the
\texttt{results\_dynamic\_uncapped.csv} precedent --- it motivates the
fix) and once after the batching commit
(\texttt{results\_dynamic\_yeco.csv}).

\subsection{The transport fix (commit \texttt{1b5da5f})}
\label{sec:fix}

\begin{sourcecode}{bash}{scripts/dist\_frames.sh --- rank collection, before and after. The same two-line change lands at the per-chunk copy in dist\_dynamic.sh.}
# before: one scp invocation -- but the SCP/SFTP protocol still moves
# files ONE AT A TIME, paying 2-3 protocol round trips per file
scp -q "$host:$wd/${DIST_PREFIX}*.png" "$DIST_OUT/"

# after: one tar stream -- files packed back-to-back on a single pipe,
# TCP stays full; -m = don't restore mtimes (matches scp's default and
# silences "time stamp in the future" from small clock skew)
ssh "$host" "cd '$wd' && tar cf - ${DIST_PREFIX}*.png" | tar xmf - -C "$DIST_OUT"
\end{sourcecode}

No compression flag: PNGs are already deflate-compressed, so \texttt{-z}
would only burn CPU on the critical path. The local (\texttt{":"}) branch
keeps its \texttt{cp}.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{The roles flip on cue: equal shares strand whichever node is slow}
\label{sec:flip}

Every equal-share config beats the laptop baseline by $30$--$47\%$
(Table~\ref{tab:static}) --- and every one loses to yeco solo by
$2.2\times$ or more. Both statements are the same mechanism report~25
measured from the other side: under equal shares each node gets
$\sim$50 frames, the laptop needs $\sim$11~s for its half where yeco
needs $2.6$--$3.4$~s, so the wall is the laptop's share and yeco idles
$\sim$75\% of the run. Block orientation replays its ivy role with the
sign flipped: the zoom's cost trend puts $\sim$70\% of the work in the
deep half, so ``block'' (deep half to the \emph{fast} node) cuts the
laptop's compute to $6.64$~s --- the built-in crude weighting, now
pointing the right way --- while ``block reversed'' inflates it to
$14.17$~s. That a $4\times$-faster remote node cannot beat its own solo
time under \emph{any} equal-share schedule is the report-25 law
verbatim: the slow node in the pair --- whichever side of the SSH link it
sits on --- buys capacity, not latency.

\subsection{The overhead ledger: better balance exposes the transport}
\label{sec:ledger}

The e2e wall of a 2-node run decomposes as
$\max(C_\ell,\; C_y + S_y)$ plus small fixed costs: the laptop's
collection is a local \texttt{cp} ($S_\ell \approx 0$), while yeco's
share must cross the WAN \emph{after} its compute ($S_y$), serially
within its rank job (\S\ref{sec:distframes}). The ``exposed overhead''
column of Table~\ref{tab:static} is this structure made visible: cyclic,
block-cyclic and reversed block all leave the laptop computing for
$11$--$14$~s, far past yeco's $2$--$3$~s finish --- yeco's shipping
completes in that shadow, and the measured exposure is only
$0.27$--$0.59$~s. The ``block'' config is the outlier that gives the
game away: the laptop finishes in $6.64$~s, the shadow shrinks, and
$4.75$~s of shipping suddenly appears in the e2e --- with $\sigma$
jumping to $2.05$~s, WAN jitter now on the critical path.

The weighted run completes the demonstration. With 1:4 shares both nodes
compute at the harmonic ideal --- laptop $4.56$~s, yeco $4.11$~s against
the analytic $4.14$ --- \textbf{and the e2e is $13.11\pm1.81$~s, worse
than equal-share cyclic's $11.24$}. Perfect balance removed the last of
the shadow: yeco's 79-file shipping ($\sim$8~s under scp) stands fully
exposed. The general statement, and the reason this never surfaced on
the LAN pair: \emph{scheduling quality and transport visibility trade
off --- the closer the schedule gets to the compute ideal, the more of
the collection cost lands on the critical path.} On ivy the same
structural exposure existed (ivy finished last and shipped last) but
cost a flat $3$--$4$~s against $40$--$60$~s walls; here it is
$\sim$8~s against a $4$~s compute ideal --- $200\%$ of the prize.

\subsection{Diagnosis: latency, not bandwidth --- 68~ms per file}
\label{sec:diagnosis}

Table~\ref{tab:transport} pins the mechanism with one controlled A/B on
the identical payload. The link moves a single 20~MB file at 30~MB/s and
the tar stream at 37~MB/s, yet scp needs $6.60$~s for 36~MB spread over
79 files --- an effective 5.5~MB/s. The arithmetic closes exactly:
$6.60 - 1.0$~s of actual bytes $= 5.6$~s of protocol over 79 files
$\approx 68$~ms/file $\approx$ 2--3 protocol round trips at the measured
25~ms RTT --- scp transfers files strictly one at a time, and each file
pays its open/write/close exchanges before the next starts. The tar pipe
packs the same files back-to-back on one stream: zero per-file round
trips, line rate, byte-identical delivery (md5-verified). In-run the
effective per-file cost is a little higher ($4.75$~s$/50$ files
$= 95$~ms in the block config's exposure) --- the micro-benchmark's warm
best case plus jitter. Neither weights nor stealing can touch this term;
it scales with the fast node's \emph{share}, which good scheduling
\emph{increases}.

\subsection{Batching realizes the ceiling: $6.45\pm0.26$~s, parity with solo-plus-fetch}
\label{sec:batching}

The identical weighted-static schedule drops $13.11 \to 6.45$~s
($-51\%$) with the transport change alone, and $\sigma$ collapses
$1.81 \to 0.26$~s --- a $7\times$ tightening that retroactively
identifies the pre-batch variance as per-file WAN jitter, not compute
noise. The residual decomposes cleanly: $\max(4.21,\; 4.19 + 1.0) + $
spec ship $+ \sim$1.2~s of fixed orchestration (GNU parallel spin-up,
per-rank \texttt{mkdir}/cleanup/\texttt{ownedFrames} round trips)
$\approx 6.4$~s --- what was measured. Against the alternatives:
$3.1\times$ the laptop baseline, and \emph{parity} with fetching a yeco
solo run's output ($5.15 + 1.4 \approx 6.5$~s) --- the delivered-to-laptop
metric that is this workflow's honest deliverable. For the third time
(ivy static, ivy dynamic, now yeco) the pair lands exactly at its
capacity-not-latency ceiling: the slow partner cannot shorten the wall,
and with the transport fixed it finally stops lengthening it. The
tightest-variance distributed config this project has measured is now
also its fastest delivered result.

\subsection{Dynamic stealing schedules lane time --- which inverts the steal direction}
\label{sec:lanetime}

Under scp, \texttt{dyn\_14}'s final split was 42/58 with the
\emph{laptop} stealing 22 frames from the bag of a node $4\times$ faster
--- backwards, by compute logic. It is correct by \emph{lane} logic: each
chunk's shipping sits between that chunk and the driver's next
\texttt{take\_chunk} (\S\ref{sec:scripts}), so a node's effective rate
is compute $+$ shipping, and the empty-bag steal rule equalizes exactly
that. Solve $\min\max\bigl(0.210\,f_\ell,\;(0.0515+0.095)\,f_y\bigr)$
with $f_\ell+f_y=100$ at the in-run 95~ms/frame shipping cost and the
optimum is 41/59 with an $8.6$~s wall: the coordinator found 42/58
unprompted, and its best rep hit $9.15$~s. The scheduler was optimizing
the true cost structure while the static weights optimized the wrong one
--- but the ``right'' answer under expensive shipping is to \emph{refuse}
the fast node's capacity, which is treating the symptom.

After batching, the lane costs converge toward pure compute and the
steal shrinks on cue (32/68, 12 stolen); the residue is yeco's remaining
per-chunk overhead --- a fresh CUDA context plus a dispatch round trip
and tar session per chunk, $\sim$0.7~s $\times$ 4 chunks on a $3.5$~s
compute lane. That is also why \texttt{dyn\_14} now costs $+21\%$ over
weighted static ($7.78$ vs.\ $6.45$~s): with calibrated weights stealing
never needs to fire, so its price is pure chunking tax. On ivy the same
insurance was free ($+0$--$1$~s) because ivy's chunks were CPU-only
processes on a LAN, and 47~s of compute dwarfed the dispatch cost.
Insurance pricing scales with per-chunk overhead over total compute ---
cheap for slow CPU nodes, expensive for fast GPU nodes.

\subsection{Wrong weights: the chunk cap now throttles the fast node}
\label{sec:capdefect}

Report~26's defect was a slow node holding an unstealable 25-frame chunk
in flight; the $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ cap fixed it by bounding
every node's in-flight exposure at the same \emph{wall-time} budget ---
\emph{assuming the weights approximate the true rates}. The 1:1 row
breaks that assumption in the opposite direction and the cap changes
sides: yeco's cap collapses to $8\cdot1=8$ frames, forcing \textbf{9
dispatches to render 63 frames} it could compute in $3.5$~s --- each
dispatch paying the CUDA context and round trips --- while the laptop
grinds its own 8-frame chunks at $\sim$2~s each. Stealing does improve
with batching (5 $\to$ 13 frames taken, e2e $15.17 \to 10.90$~s), but
the recovery is nothing like ivy's 72\%: the fast node reaches its
empty-bag steal trigger late (its cadence is overhead-throttled), and
in-flight chunks plus the victim's own steady drain leave only a thin
tail to take. The structural statement: $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ bounds
exposure in wall time only when $w_r \propto$ true rate --- miscalibrated
weights corrupt the cap along with the shares. The fix is to stop
trusting the operator's number twice: estimate each node's rate from its
first measured chunk and re-derive caps (and steal sizes) from that.

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Keep collection batched (done, commit \texttt{1b5da5f}).}
        One tar stream per rank: $6.9\times$ on the transfer itself,
        $-51\%$ on the weighted e2e wall, $\sigma$ $\times7$ tighter.
        It also helps the LAN pair (fewer round trips), just less.
        Trade-off: none measured --- delivery is byte-identical and the
        \texttt{-m} flag reproduces scp's mtime behaviour.
  \item \textbf{On WAN pairs, use weighted static with measured
        weights; treat dynamic stealing as paid insurance.} Weighted
        static 1:4 delivers $6.45\pm0.26$~s; stealing costs $+21\%$ when
        weights are right (chunking tax on a fast GPU node). Buy it when
        weights are uncertain; skip it when they are measured.
  \item \textbf{Weights belong to the (machine, executor-mix,
        transport) triple.} Report~26 showed executor-mix dependence
        (5:1 $\to$ 2.4:1 when CPU-pinned); this report adds transport
        dependence --- under scp the lane-optimal split was 41/59, under
        tar it is $\sim$24/76. Recalibrate after any transport change.
  \item \textbf{Rate-adaptive chunk caps (follow-up).} Derive each
        node's cap and steal size from its first chunk's measured rate
        instead of the operator's weights ($\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ is
        only as good as $w_r$; \S\ref{sec:capdefect} shows it throttling
        a $4\times$-fast node to 8-frame chunks). Expected effect: the
        \texttt{dyn\_11} row approaches the \texttt{dyn\_14} one without
        operator input.
  \item \textbf{If sub-$6.5$~s delivered matters, the next $\sim$1.2~s
        is fixed orchestration} (parallel spin-up, per-rank
        \texttt{mkdir}/cleanup round trips, spec ship) --- amortizable
        by keeping rank workdirs warm between runs. Below that sits the
        $4.14$~s harmonic ideal, and nothing on this pair goes lower.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Rerunning reports 25--26 with the speed roles reversed confirmed the
distribution law from the other side --- equal shares strand whichever
node is slow, and no schedule beats the fast node's solo throughput ---
and then surfaced the term the LAN pair had kept invisible: per-file
collection latency, $\sim$68~ms/file over an SSH jump, $\sim$8~s on a
79-file share. It is a cost that grows exactly when scheduling improves,
which is why the compute-ideal weighted split measured \emph{worse}
end-to-end than naive equal shares until the transport was fixed. One
tar stream per rank removed it: weighted static 1:4 went
$13.11\pm1.81 \to 6.45\pm0.26$~s, parity with fetching the fast node's
solo output --- the pair's analytic ceiling, realized with the tightest
variance of any distributed config measured in this project. The dynamic
coordinator, unmodified, priced the same phenomenon from a different
angle: it equalizes lane time (compute $+$ shipping), stealing
\emph{into} the slow node under scp and shrinking that steal after
batching --- and with calibrated weights its chunking overhead now makes
it strictly insurance, $+21\%$ on a fast GPU node where ivy's CPU chunks
had been free. Wrong weights still cost real money because the
weight-proportional chunk cap inherits their error; rate-adaptive caps
are the identified follow-up. The homogeneous interleave study
(report~13's earmark) remains open --- both measured pairs are too
asymmetric for interleave effects to be visible.
