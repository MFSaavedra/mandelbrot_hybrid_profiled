\begin{abstract}
Report \texttt{08-region-metrics-viz.tex} pinned the binding load-balancing
outliers to minibrot interiors: $960\times540$ regions whose four
corners all reach \texttt{maxIter} (spread 0), so the 4-corner uniformity rule
never subdivides them. This report implements the next-step~2b remedy --- a
9-point stencil that augments the four corners with the four edge
midpoints and the centre --- and measures it against the 4-corner baseline on
100~frames at $1920\times1080$ (i7-9750H + GTX~1660~Ti~Max-Q, 12~threads,
\texttt{diffT}\,=\,0.1, \texttt{save}\,=\,0). The headline is a partial
win: the 9-point stencil exposes +5.4\,\% more leaves (5{,}323~$\to$~5{,}608)
and eliminates the frame-89 outlier (84.4\,\% interior), cutting the
count of $>$5~s regions from 2 to 1. But the densest region ---
frame~73, 97.3\,\% interior --- survives unchanged at 13.3~s, because
all nine stencil points still land at \texttt{maxIter}. Crucially, end-to-end
wall time is statistically unchanged: 52.39~s vs.\ 52.07~s over 3~reps
each ($+0.6\%$, inside the $\sim$1\,\% run-to-run spread). A Nsight Systems pass
explains why, and corrects an earlier guess: the extra sampling costs a
measured $+0.40$~s of CPU ($+29$~µs per \texttt{examine()},
$\sim$37~ms wall-equivalent) --- negligible, not an ``offset'' --- and splitting
conserves total compute ($\approx$595~s in both binaries). The
heuristic moves work between threads but removes none; with the load already
balanced under 2\,\%, the wall is bound by total interior-pixel work. The next
optimisation must therefore reduce that work (periodicity checking or an
interior certificate), not subdivide it further.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Setup and the Change Measured}

\paragraph{Workload and machine.} Identical to report~08: the same
\texttt{spec.in} (100~frames, $1920\times1080$, zoom ending at
\texttt{maxIterations}\,=\,10{,}000), the same i7-9750H (6c/12t) +
GTX~1660~Ti~Max-Q, 12~worker threads (1~GPU~+~11~CPU),
\texttt{diffThreshold}\,=\,0.1, \texttt{pixelSizeThresh}\,=\,32{,}768,
\texttt{save}\,=\,0 for pure-compute timing.

\paragraph{The change.} The 4-corner uniformity test in
\texttt{MandelRegion::examine()} is extended to a 3$\times$3 stencil: the four
corners (as before) plus the four edge midpoints and the centre. The minimum
and maximum iteration counts --- and therefore the
\texttt{(maxIter - minIter) < diffThresh * maxIter} decision --- are taken over
all nine points.

\begin{sourcecode}{cpp}{mandelregion.cpp --- the five points added to the uniformity test.}
double midX = (upperX + lowerX) * 0.5;
double midY = (upperY + lowerY) * 0.5;
int extra[5] = {
  diverge (midX,   upperY),   // top edge midpoint
  diverge (midX,   lowerY),   // bottom edge midpoint
  diverge (upperX, midY),     // left edge midpoint
  diverge (lowerX, midY),     // right edge midpoint
  diverge (midX,   midY)      // centre
};
for (int i = 0; i < 5; i++) {
  if (extra[i] < minIter) minIter = extra[i];
  if (extra[i] > maxIter) maxIter = extra[i];
}
\end{sourcecode}

A child still inherits only its one shared outer corner (unchanged), so the
five interior points are always recomputed: each \texttt{examine()} adds five
\texttt{diverge()} calls over the 4-corner version. The split geometry and all
other logic are untouched.

\paragraph{Baseline and measurement.} The 4-corner baseline is
\texttt{binary-v2-viz} built in a separate worktree. Four measurement passes
were taken on both binaries: (i) a clean wall-time A/B, three reps each,
alternating to balance thermal drift; (ii) a single \texttt{quiet}\,=\,0
metrics run for leaf counts, per-thread totals and the \texttt{[OUTLIER]} dump;
(iii) a Nsight Systems capture
(\texttt{nsys profile --trace=cuda,nvtx}) for the NVTX range breakdown, the
CUDA API summary and the CUPTI kernel/mem-op statistics; and (iv) a
\texttt{quiet}\,=\,0 run whose per-region stderr lines give the full
compute-time distribution. The \texttt{osrt} trace was omitted (OS-thread
scheduling was not under analysis), keeping the capture lean.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{The stencil catches the 84\%-interior outlier but not the 97\% one}

This is the headline. Both outliers have four corners pinned at \texttt{maxIter}
(spread 0 under the 4-corner rule). The 9-point stencil adds five interior
samples; whether it splits the region depends on whether any of those
five lands on a pixel that diverges before \texttt{maxIter}.

For frame~89 (84.4\,\% interior) at least one interior sample fell on the
$\sim$15.6\,\% of the region that is outside the minibrot, producing a non-zero
spread and forcing the split --- the region disappears from the outlier list.
For frame~73 (97.3\,\% interior) all nine points landed on the $\sim$97\,\% that
is inside, so the spread stayed 0 and the region computed as a single 13.3~s
block. The mechanism is sparse sampling: the stencil catches a filament only
when the filament crosses one of nine fixed points, and the densest interior
regions have so little divergent area that thin filaments slip between the
samples. The implication is that 9-point is a probabilistic improvement, not a
guarantee --- its hit rate falls as interior density rises.

\subsection{More leaves, smaller GPU regions}

The stencil exposed $+5.4\%$ leaves (Table~\ref{tab:decomp}) by detecting
interior spread that the corners missed at many regions, not just the
outliers. The GPU absorbed most of the new fine-grained work (its average
region fell $11.56\to11.01$~ms, and it claimed $+136$ leaves), consistent with
the report-04 observation that the GPU drains small regions fastest. The
$+95$ extra splits are the new internal nodes feeding that growth.

\subsection{Why wall time does not move --- and it is not the sampling cost}

Despite removing a 15~s outlier and shifting the whole CPU distribution down
(Table~\ref{tab:dist}: median $126\to99$~ms, p99 $4247\to3833$~ms, max
$15536\to13426$~ms), the A/B wall time is flat: $+0.32$~s ($+0.6\%$), with the
reps' ranges overlapping (9-point's fastest rep, 52.10~s, beats 4-point's
slowest, 52.34~s). The Nsight Systems capture lets us say precisely why, and
correct an earlier guess.

\paragraph{The sampling overhead is negligible --- it does not offset the
gain.} An earlier draft of this report attributed the flat result partly to the
cost of the five extra samples. The NVTX breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:nvtx})
refutes that: the non-compute overhead rises only from 3.49~s to 3.89~s summed
across eleven threads --- +0.40~s, or +29~µs per \texttt{examine()} ---
about 37~ms of wall-equivalent. That is two orders of magnitude too small to
explain a flat wall. The five extra \texttt{diverge()} calls are simply cheap
next to the pixel loop they guard.

\paragraph{Splitting conserves total work; the regime is throughput-bound.} The
real reason is in the same table: total compute (CPU+GPU pixel loops) is
unchanged at $\approx$595--600~s ($+0.9\%$, noise). Every pixel is iterated
exactly once however the frame is partitioned, so subdividing a region
redistributes work without reducing it. With eleven CPU workers and
100~frames in flight, no single region is on the critical path --- while one
thread spends 13~s on the frame-73 block, the other ten drain the remaining
frames --- so the wall is governed by total work over parallelism
($\approx$595~s~/~11~$+$~GPU help $\approx$~52~s), which 9-point leaves
unchanged. The improved per-region distribution would only translate into wall
time if a single region bound the critical path, which it does not at this
thread count.

\paragraph{The GPU path is untouched.} Table~\ref{tab:cuda} confirms the kernel
total (45.5~s), the kernel max (137~ms), and the \texttt{cudaMemcpy}
99\,\%-kernel-wait signature are all unchanged; 9-point only shifts the GPU
toward more, slightly smaller launches (avg $11.63\to10.78$~ms over
$3{,}910\to4{,}228$ instances) with the same tail. The actual
D$\rightarrow$H transfer is $\sim$95~ms total --- still not a bottleneck.

\subsection{The binding cost is work, not balance}

The decisive insight from the breakdown is that the heuristic can only move
where work runs, never how much there is. The frame-73 block
costs $960\times540 \times {\approx}7{,}300$ iterations $\approx 3.8\times10^9$
iterations no matter how it is split; 9-point's finer decomposition cannot
remove a single iteration. The load was already well balanced (per-thread
spread under 2\,\% in both samplers), so improving balance further buys nothing.
The only lever that can shrink the $\sim$595~s of total compute is one that
skips iterating interior pixels --- an interior certificate that
constant-fills a region proven to be inside the set. This is why the wall is
flat and why the next optimisation must target work, not subdivision.

\subsection{Where 9-point would pay off}

The flat result is configuration-specific. In a latency-bound setup --- CPU-only,
or a GPU-starved hybrid where a single 15~s region is the critical path
--- subdividing that region across threads would translate directly into wall
time. The 9-point stencil's value here is therefore decomposition correctness
(it makes the heuristic see interior structure) plus insurance for
configurations where the long tail binds, not a throughput win at this thread
count.

\section{Re-Tuning Check: \texttt{diffThreshold} on the 9-Point Binary}

Because the 9-point stencil makes the heuristic split more readily, the
performance-optimal \texttt{diffThreshold} could in principle have moved from the
0.1 that \texttt{06-difft-sweep.tex} found best for the 4-corner sampler. To
check, the hybrid configuration was swept across
\texttt{diffT}\,$\in\{0.05,0.10,0.20,0.30,0.50\}$, three reps each, on the
9-point binary (same machine, 100~frames, \texttt{pixT}\,=\,32{,}768,
\texttt{save}\,=\,0); data in \texttt{experiments/10-difft-9point/}.

The optimum did not move --- 0.1 remains the right default. The leaf
count saturates by \texttt{diffT}\,=\,0.1: 0.05 produces the identical
5{,}608 leaves, because the \texttt{pixelSizeThresh} floor is reached and a lower
threshold can add no further splits. Across the saturated band
\texttt{diffT}\,$\in\{0.05,0.10,0.20\}$ the wall times are statistically
indistinguishable (51--52~s, with single-rep ranges that overlap and exceed the
$\sim$1~s separation of their means); only 0.30 ($+{\sim}1$~s, 5{,}581 leaves)
and 0.50 ($+{\sim}1.6$~s, 5{,}491 leaves) are measurably worse as splitting
drops off. \texttt{diffT}\,=\,0.05 has the lowest mean but the widest spread
(2.3~s) --- noisy, not reliably faster --- while 0.20 is the steadiest; 0.1 sits
squarely in the flat optimum.

This reproduces the 4-corner result of \texttt{06-difft-sweep.tex} --- a plateau
over [0.05, 0.20] with 0.1 the predictable sweet spot --- and is exactly what
the analysis above predicts: because the wall is throughput-bound and total
compute is conserved, \texttt{diffThreshold} only sets partition granularity,
which saturates at the pixel floor by $\sim$0.1 for either sampler. The
change from 4 to 9 sampling points does not warrant re-tuning the default.
(Hybrid config only; CPU-only and GPU-only were not swept.)

\section{Recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Keep 9-point as the default sampler. It strictly improves
    decomposition quality --- one of two binding outliers eliminated, CPU
    per-region median $-21\%$ and max $-14\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:dist}) --- at a
    measured cost of $+0.40$~s CPU total ($+29$~µs/\texttt{examine}, $\sim$37~ms
    wall-equivalent; Table~\ref{tab:nvtx}). It is the correct heuristic, and it
    is effectively free. Keep its \texttt{diffThreshold} at 0.1 --- the sweep of
    Table~\ref{tab:difft} confirms the optimum did not move with the stencil
    change.

    \item Attack work, not balance --- this is the real lever. The
    breakdown shows total compute is conserved under any partitioning
    (Table~\ref{tab:nvtx}); the only way to shrink the $\sim$595~s is to stop
    iterating interior pixels. Two options, in order of safety:
    (a)~periodicity checking inside \texttt{diverge()} --- detect the
    cycle that interior orbits settle into and bail early; exact, no image
    change, and it directly cuts the frame-73 block's $3.8\times10^9$ iterations;
    (b)~a heuristic interior certificate that constant-fills a large
    region whose samples all reach \texttt{maxIter} --- cheaper still but risks
    filling over a thin filament, so it trades a small accuracy risk for speed.
    The exact cardioid/bulb certificate (report~08) does not apply --- frame~73
    is a minibrot interior.

    \item Re-test 9-point in a latency-bound config. Run CPU-only and
    GPU+1CPU; where a single region bounds the critical path, 9-point's tighter
    distribution (and the residual-outlier split of recommendation~2a) should
    convert into wall time that is invisible at 12~threads.

    \item Then the throughput levers of report~08 (async streams,
    priority queue) --- but note Table~\ref{tab:cuda}: the D$\rightarrow$H
    transfer is only $\sim$95~ms, so async streams recover GPU-thread idle
    around the kernel, not transfer time.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The 9-point stencil does what report~08 predicted it would for the
less-dense outlier: frame~89, 84\,\% interior, is detected and
subdivided, dropping the count of $>$5~s regions from two to one and exposing
$+5.4\%$ more leaves overall. It does not fix the densest region ---
frame~73, 97\,\% interior --- whose thin divergent filament slips between all
nine fixed sample points; sparse sampling is a probabilistic catch, not a
guarantee. And at \texttt{diffT}\,=\,0.1 on this 12-thread hybrid the change is
wall-time-neutral ($+0.6\%$, within noise). The Nsight Systems breakdown
pins down why: the extra sampling costs a measured $+0.40$~s of CPU
($\sim$37~ms wall-equivalent), far too little to matter, and splitting
conserves total compute ($\approx$595~s in both, $+0.9\%$) --- it moves
work between threads but removes none. Since the load was already balanced to
under 2\,\%, better decomposition has nothing left to gain at this thread count.
The 9-point sampler is the right default on correctness grounds and is
effectively free, but the wall is bound by total interior-pixel work, so the
next optimisation must reduce that work --- periodicity checking or an
interior certificate (recommendation~2) --- rather than subdivide it further.
