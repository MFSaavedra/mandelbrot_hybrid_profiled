\begin{abstract}
Reports 26--27 set the static frame shares from node compute speed alone
(solo walls, $21.02/5.15 = 4.08 \to$ weights 1:4) and then repaired the
consequences empirically. This report feeds the same measured numbers into
the machinery chapter~11 of the course text builds for exactly this
problem --- collection-aware divisible load theory, via the book's own
DLTlib solver --- and checks the theory against everything the campaign
measured. It retrodicts all of it. \textbf{With the communication term
deleted ($l=0$) the closed form reproduces our guessed weights (80.3/19.7
$\approx$ 1:4); with the measured per-file scp cost inserted
($l=83.5$~ms/frame) the optimum shifts to 60.9/39.1 and the model prices
our 1:4 partition at 11.9~s --- the collection wall of report~27,
predicted}. The work-stealer's ``backwards'' scp equilibrium (42/58) lands
within 3 frames of the DLT optimum (39/61): lane-time self-scheduling
\emph{is} the model's finish-time equalization, found by iteration instead
of algebra. Post-batching ($l=12.2$~ms) the optimum is 76.7/23.3 and our
1:4 sits $0.14$~s ($2\%$) from it --- inside $\sigma=0.26$, so no
re-weighting is warranted. The model also ranks the levers the way
report~27 concluded: perfectly re-weighted scp (9.4~s) still loses to
naively-weighted tar (6.2~s) --- transport beats scheduling in closed
form. Limits: the load is linear only \emph{on average}, and it is the
seeded random interleave of \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS} that makes it so.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation: we optimized two of the three terms}
\label{sec:motivation}

Every static share this project has assigned was derived from compute
speed alone. Report~26 set laptop:ivy at 5:1 from per-frame throughput;
report~27 set laptop:yeco at 1:4 from the solo walls. Both campaigns then
hit communication-shaped problems --- the unstealable in-flight chunk, the
exposed collection wall --- and fixed them empirically (the
$\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ cap, tar-stream batching). Chapter~11 of the
course text derives the static-partitioning answer \emph{with} the
communication terms: an affine cost model per node
(compute $p_i(part_i L + e_i)$, distribution $l_i(a\,part_i L + b)$,
collection $l_i(c\,part_i L + d)$; Eqs.~11.5/11.7/11.8), closed-form
optima whose shares carry the link cost in the denominator
($part_i \propto 1/(l_i(a{+}c) + p_i)$, Eq.~11.10), sequencing theorems
for 1-port platforms, and a library --- DLTlib --- that evaluates all of
it. The question this report answers: \emph{had we used the book's model
from the start, what would it have prescribed, and does it explain what we
measured?} It is the closing bracket of the campaign: the dynamic
load-balancing case study (this codebase is \S11.5.1 of the same chapter)
rediscovering the static-LB half of its own chapter from the other
direction.

\section{Model and calibration}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Mapping the frame distribution onto the DLT cost model}

The two-level design maps directly onto a single-level DLT star: the load
$L=100$ frames is arbitrarily divisible at frame granularity, the laptop
is the load-originating root that also computes, yeco is a worker behind
one link. Three observations fix the model constants:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Distribution is free at the margin} ($a=0$). Every node
        reads the same $\sim$200-byte \texttt{spec.in} and derives its
        frames from the ownership predicate; shipping the spec is a
        per-run constant, not a per-frame cost. (It is folded into the
        1.2~s fixed orchestration term below, together with the GNU
        parallel spin-up and per-rank \texttt{mkdir}/cleanup round trips
        measured in report~27.)
  \item \textbf{Collection is proportional} ($c=1$, $l$ = per-frame PNG
        cost). Output volume scales with the share:
        $\sim$0.46~MB/frame. The laptop's own frames end as a local
        \texttt{cp}, so its link cost is 0 --- the book makes the same
        $l_0 = 0$ assumption for the master (p.~903).
  \item \textbf{Node speed is the solo wall over 100} ---
        $p_\ell = 0.2102$, $p_y = 0.0515$~s/frame --- exactly the book's
        prescription of calibrating $p$ by regression on a timing run
        (\S11.5.2 does it for AES encryption; our ``regression'' has one
        point per node because cost is linear in frames by construction).
\end{itemize}

The link parameter is the book's ping-pong measurement in our clothes:
report~27's transfer micro-A/B on the identical 79-file/36~MB payload
gives $l_{\mathrm{scp}} = 6.60/79 = 83.5$~ms/frame and
$l_{\mathrm{tar}} = 0.96/79 = 12.2$~ms/frame. That $l$ changed by
$6.9\times$ when only the \emph{protocol} changed is the campaign's
central fact restated in model terms: $l$ belongs to the (path, protocol)
pair, not to the wire.

\subsection{The solver}

DLTlib's \texttt{Network::SolveImage(L, a, c)} implements the
collection-aware ``image processing'' problem class --- distribution
$a \cdot l \cdot part_i L$, collection $c \cdot l \cdot part_i L$ ---
which is our shape exactly (the \texttt{ImageQuery} family models
\emph{constant-size} collection, the wrong shape for PNG streams). The
driver builds the star, solves at each transport's $l$, and also sweeps
$l \in [0, 100]$~ms in 1~ms steps for
Fig.~\ref{fig:model}. One scope limit: \texttt{ImageAggregate} reads a
single link speed per parent (\texttt{temp->link[0]}), so the mixed
WAN$+$LAN three-node star is evaluated with the hand closed form the
2-node solves validate ($n_i \propto 1/(p_i + l_i)$, $l_\ell = 0$).

\begin{sourcecode}{cpp}{experiments/28-dlt-analysis/mandel\_dlt.cpp --- the 2-node solve. DLTlib is unity-included, DLTdemo-style; run.sh builds it against a patched copy of the external library.}
Network net;
net.InsertNode((char *)"laptop", P_LAPTOP, 0, (char *)NULL, 0, true);
net.InsertNode((char *)"yeco",   P_YECO,   0, (char *)"laptop", link, true);
net.SolveImage(L, 0.0, 1.0);   // a = 0 (spec is free), c = 1 (PNGs)
double part0 = net.head->part;             // laptop's share
double part1 = net.head->child[0]->part;   // yeco's share
double wall  = P_LAPTOP * part0 * L;       // root computes start-to-finish
\end{sourcecode}

Fixed partitions (our \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS} 1:4) are evaluated under the
same cost model as
$\max\bigl(p_\ell n_\ell,\; (p_y + l)\, n_y\bigr)$; all predictions add
the 1.2~s fixed orchestration constant measured in report~27. Measured
comparisons use the report-27 CSVs
(\texttt{results\_dynamic\_yeco\{\_scp,\}.csv}).

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Setting $l=0$ reproduces the guessed weights: we ran DLT with the communication term deleted}
\label{sec:lzero}

The compute-only optimum is 80.3/19.7 (Table~\ref{tab:dlt}, row~1) ---
our 1:4 weights to within rounding. This is not a coincidence to admire:
with $l=0$ the closed form degenerates to
$part_i \propto 1/p_i$, which \emph{is} the solo-wall ratio rule we used.
The implication runs the other way: every consequence the campaign then
measured --- the 13.11~s collection wall, the $\sigma = 1.81$ WAN jitter,
the stealer ``rescuing'' the schedule --- is the model's predicted cost of
the deleted term, $l \cdot part_y \cdot L = 0.0835 \times 79 = 6.6$~s of
exposed shipping on a 4.4~s compute lane. Report~27 discovered
empirically that ``balanced compute exposes collection''; Eq.~11.8 had
carried that term all along.

\subsection{The model prices the levers the way report 27 concluded: transport beats scheduling}
\label{sec:pricing}

Under scp the DLT optimum is 39.1/60.9 at a predicted 9.42~s ---
re-weighting alone would have recovered $2.4$~s of our 11.87~s. Batching
alone (our naive 1:4 under tar) predicts 6.23~s. \textbf{The perfectly
re-weighted scp schedule loses to the naively weighted tar schedule by
3.2~s}: the reason is structural, visible in panel (b) of
Fig.~\ref{fig:model} --- under expensive shipping the optimum
\emph{refuses} the fast node's capacity (its share falls to 61), so the
platform's aggregate rate drops; cutting $l$ restores the capacity
instead of rationing it. This is report~27's ``the actionable lever is
collection batching, not scheduling'' derived rather than measured ---
and it generalizes: on this pair, one round of protocol engineering was
worth more than any amount of scheduling optimality.

\subsection{The work-stealer converged to the DLT optimum it had never heard of}
\label{sec:stealer}

Report~27 explained the scp-era steal inversion --- the \emph{slow}
laptop stealing 22 frames from a $4\times$ faster node --- as lane-time
self-scheduling: each chunk's shipping sits inside the driver's dispatch
loop, so the empty-bag rule equalizes compute$+$shipping. The DLT solve
makes that explanation exact: finish-time equalization over lanes of rate
$p_\ell$ and $p_y + l$ is precisely the model's optimality condition, and
its scp answer is 39/61 --- the coordinator landed at 42/58, within 3
frames, by iteration instead of algebra. The modeled walls agree to
$0.6$~s (10.03 predicted at 42/58 vs.\ 9.44 at the optimum; measured
$12.95 \pm 3.45$, the gap being WAN jitter and per-chunk overhead the
static model does not carry). The implication: the dynamic dispenser is
not a rival to the static theory --- it is a runtime \emph{estimator} of
the same optimum, worth its $+21\%$ chunking premium exactly when the
model's inputs ($p_i$, $l_i$) are unknown or wrong.

\subsection{Post-batching, the guessed weights are 2\% from optimal --- re-weighting is not warranted}
\label{sec:tarok}

At $l = 12.2$~ms the optimum shifts to 76.7/23.3 and predicts 6.09~s; our
1:4 evaluates to 6.23~s. The 0.14~s gap is $2\%$ of the wall and half of
the measured $\sigma = 0.26$~s --- unresolvable by a 3-rep benchmark, and
smaller than the day-to-day thermal drift of the laptop baseline. This
closes the calibration question report~27 left open: after the transport
fix, the solo-wall weights are effectively optimal, and the collection
term, while real, is now too small to justify a second calibration knob.
The general rule falls out of panel (a): the fixed-weights error grows
with $l$, so \emph{compute-only weights are safe exactly when the
transport is batched}.

\subsection{The three-node outlook: ivy is worth 4.8 frames and 0.24 s --- capacity, not latency, in closed form}
\label{sec:threenode}

Adding ivy ($p = 0.958$~s/frame, LAN link $l \approx 4$~ms) to the
tar-era pair, the lane-equalization optimum hands it 4.8 of 100 frames
and shortens the predicted compute wall from 4.89 to 4.65~s ---
$-0.24$~s, smaller than the measured $\sigma$ of the 2-node config it
would improve. The model thus reproduces the campaign's thrice-realized
ceiling as arithmetic: a node contributes rate
$1/(p_i + l_i)$, ivy's is $1.04$~s$^{-1}\cdot$frames against the pair's
$20.5$, i.e., $4.8\%$ more capacity at the price of a third machine, its
orchestration, and its failure modes. The book's negative-$part_i$
remark (p.~903: nodes whose share solves negative should be dropped ---
``finding the optimum set of nodes'') is the same judgment in the limit;
ivy's share is positive but its \emph{value} is sub-noise. DLT answers
``should this node join?'' before any benchmark is run.

\subsection{Validity: the load is linear only on average --- and it is the random interleave that makes it so}
\label{sec:validity}

DLT's premise is an arbitrarily divisible load with uniform per-unit
cost. Our frames violate it individually: the zoom's cost trend puts
$\sim$70\% of the work in the deep half (reports 25/27's block-orientation
effect, a $3.4$~s swing between ``block'' and ``block reversed''). The
model applies anyway because \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS}' seeded
weighted-random assignment samples that trend uniformly --- every node's
\emph{expected} per-frame cost is the global mean, which is exactly the
$p_i$ the solo walls calibrate. Contiguous block assignment breaks the
premise and would need per-frame cost profiles; the interleave is what
buys the right to use four constants instead. Remaining model debts:
the 1.2~s orchestration constant is measured, not derived; per-chunk
overheads of the dynamic mode (CUDA context, dispatch round trips) are
outside the static model; and the 1-port sequencing theory
(Theorem~11.3.3, sort by $b_i(p_i + l)$) is vacuous at one remote link
but binds the moment a second remote node shares the laptop's uplink.

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Set static weights by $1/(p_i + l_i)$, not $1/p_i$.} Same
        measurement effort (one solo wall $+$ one transfer A/B per node),
        strictly more correct. On the batched pair it is worth only
        $0.14$~s ($2\%$, sub-$\sigma$); on any higher-latency transport
        the gap grows like panel (b)'s curve spread --- $2.4$~s at scp's
        $l$.
  \item \textbf{Run the closed form as a pre-flight before adding a
        node.} Ten lines of arithmetic ($n_i \propto 1/(p_i + l_i)$,
        wall $= n_0 p_0 +$ fixed) predicts the achievable e2e and each
        node's marginal value before any distributed run: it prices ivy
        at $-0.24$~s (skip) and would have priced the entire report-25
        equal-share sweep as strictly dominated. The driver in
        \texttt{experiments/28-dlt-analysis/} is the reusable template.
  \item \textbf{Recalibrate $l$ on any transport change; keep $p$ on any
        executor-mix change.} Report~26 showed weights belong to the
        (machine, executor-mix) pair; this report shows the $6.9\times$
        protocol dependence of $l$. The triple of report~27's
        recommendation~3 is now two independent knobs with separate
        cheap calibrations.
  \item \textbf{Treat the dynamic dispenser as the estimator of this
        model's inputs} (\S\ref{sec:stealer}) --- its planned
        rate-adaptive chunk caps are precisely a runtime fit of
        $1/(p_i + l_i)$ per node, the book's two-phase ``runtime model
        calculation'' idea (\S11.3.1). When the fit is already known,
        weighted static wins by the $+21\%$ chunking premium.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Fed the campaign's own measured constants, the chapter-11 model
retrodicts every headline of reports 25--27: our guessed 1:4 weights are
its $l=0$ solution (80.3/19.7); the collection wall is the deleted
$l$-term's predicted 6.6~s; the stealer's ``backwards'' 42/58 equilibrium
is 3 frames from the closed-form 39/61 optimum; the batched-era 1:4 sits
$2\%$ (sub-$\sigma$) from optimal, so the empirical calibration stands;
and batching dominates re-weighting (6.23 vs.\ 9.42~s) because a cheaper
link restores capacity that an optimal schedule can only ration. The
model also prices the next decision for free --- ivy joining the batched
pair buys $0.24$~s, below noise --- and its premise (uniform per-unit
cost) is satisfied not by the workload but by our own seeded random
interleave, which is why four constants suffice. What the theory does not
carry --- per-chunk CUDA contexts, WAN jitter, the 1.2~s orchestration
floor --- is exactly the list of residuals reports 26--27 measured. The
campaign closes as a loop: the codebase is the chapter's dynamic-LB case
study, and its distribution layer, tuned empirically, arrived where the
same chapter's static-LB theory had been pointing all along.
