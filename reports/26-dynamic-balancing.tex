\begin{abstract}
Report~25 ended with an analytic verdict: on the laptop+ivy pair
($4.56\times$ per-frame throughput gap), no static equal-share assignment
can beat the laptop alone, and even a perfect weighted split is bounded at
$1.22\times$ ($15.4$~s) with $3.4$--$4.2$~s of orchestration overhead eating
half of that --- so \emph{parity} is the realistic target. This report
builds the two schedulers that verdict calls for and measures them. A
seeded weighted-random static assignment (\texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS}, splitmix64
over the frame index --- communication-free, identical on every machine) and
a coordinator-driven work-stealing mode (guided chunks through the new
\texttt{DIST\_FRAMES} list interface; an empty node steals half the richest
undispatched tail). Both are \textbf{100/100 byte-identical} at production
scale, including a live corrective steal. With calibrated 5:1 weights both
reach \textbf{baseline parity} ($22.93\pm0.11$ and $22.01\pm0.56$~s vs.\ a
$21.06\pm2.73$~s baseline; equal-share static: $46.90$~s). With deliberately
wrong 1:1 weights, stealing recovers \textbf{72\%} of the miscalibration
penalty autonomously ($46.90 \to 28.18\pm0.37$~s) --- but only after fixing
the defect the first run exposed: an in-flight chunk is unstealable, so
chunks must be capped at $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ frames to bound every
node's exposure at the same wall-time budget.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Motivation: what report 25 left on the table}
\label{sec:motivation}

The equal-share benchmark of \texttt{25-frame-distribution.tex} was a clean
negative: cyclic assignment ran $+150\%$ against the laptop alone because
both nodes got $\sim$50 frames and ivy needs $0.855$~s per frame where the
laptop hybrid needs $0.188$~s. Its analysis derived exactly what a better
scheduler is worth on this pair: the combined-throughput ideal is $15.4$~s
($1.22\times$), the fast node should carry $\sim$82\% of the frames, and
$3.4$--$4.2$~s of ship/collect overhead consumes most of the ideal gain.
Two mechanisms follow from that arithmetic, and this report implements and
measures both:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item a \textbf{static weighted} assignment --- give each node a share
        proportional to its speed, randomly interleaved so every share
        samples the zoom's $32\times$ cost trend uniformly (the weighted
        analogue of cyclic); and
  \item \textbf{dynamic work stealing} on top --- when a node is about to
        finish, it pulls frames from the loaded node, covering the case
        where the weights are wrong (they are configuration-dependent:
        \S\ref{sec:corrective} shows the same pair at $2.4\times$, not
        $4.56\times$, when the laptop is CPU-pinned).
\end{enumerate}

\section{The change: two ownership modes and a stealing coordinator}
\label{sec:change}

\paragraph{Weighted-random static (\texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS}, commit
\texttt{ffe61aa}).} A third ownership mode joins report~25's block-cyclic
predicate in \texttt{main.cpp} (precedence: \texttt{DIST\_FRAMES} $>$
\texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS} $>$ block-cyclic):

\begin{sourcecode}{cpp}{main.cpp --- the weighted-random owner draw (abridged).}
// SplitMix64: the weighted-random frame owner must be computed
// identically by every rank on every machine and compiler, so the
// draw is pure uint64 arithmetic -- no floating point, no rand().
unsigned long long h =
  splitmix64 (distSeed + (unsigned long long) i)
  % (unsigned long long) distWeightSum;
owned = h >= (unsigned long long) distWeightLo
     && h <  (unsigned long long) distWeightHi;
\end{sourcecode}

Frame $i$ belongs to the rank whose cumulative-weight interval
$[\mathrm{lo}_r, \mathrm{hi}_r)$ contains the hash --- with
\texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS=5,1}, rank~0 owns each frame with probability $5/6$.
Because the draw is a pure integer function of \texttt{(seed, i)}, every
rank on every machine computes the \emph{same partition} with no
communication, preserving report~25's collision-free-union property; and
because it is (hash-)random in $i$, each share is an unbiased sample of the
cost trend. Everything downstream is untouched.

\paragraph{The explicit-list mode (\texttt{DIST\_FRAMES}).} The second
addition is the dispenser interface report~25's seam predicted:
\texttt{DIST\_FRAMES="3,17,42"} renders exactly those global indices
(range-checked, no \texttt{DIST\_NODES}/\texttt{RANK} needed). It is the
primitive the dynamic coordinator drives.

\paragraph{The stealing coordinator (\texttt{scripts/dist\_dynamic.sh}).}
One process on the coordinator; no node-to-node sockets; the intra-node
region queue untouched. The frame list is shuffled with a seeded
deterministic shuffle and dealt proportionally to \texttt{WEIGHTS} into
per-rank \emph{bags}. Each node's driver loops: take a guided chunk from its
own bag --- half the remaining frames, at least \texttt{KMIN} (4), at most
$\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ (\S\ref{sec:cap}) --- run one
\texttt{DIST\_FRAMES} invocation on the node, collect its PNGs, repeat.
When a node's bag is empty --- it is about to finish --- the driver
\textbf{steals} half the richest node's undispatched tail (same caps) and
keeps rendering. A single \texttt{flock} serializes all bag mutations, and
an in-flight chunk is already out of every bag, so each frame is rendered
exactly once; guided halving keeps a node's own share at
$\sim\log_2(\mathrm{share}/\mathrm{KMIN})$ process startups.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Same pair, spec, and discipline as \texttt{25-frame-distribution.tex}: node
A = laptop (i7-9750H + GTX 1660 Ti, hybrid \texttt{12 1 0.1 32768 1 1}),
node B = ivy (i7-4702MQ, \texttt{make GPU=0}, CPU-only
\texttt{8 0 0.1 32768 1 1}); production spec (100 frames,
$1920\times1080$), \texttt{save}\,=\,1, both machines on AC (gated by the
driver), 3 reps per configuration.

\paragraph{Identity.} Executor-pinned (both nodes CPU-only), mirroring
report~25 \S3: weighted static 5:1 and dynamic-with-stealing 5:1, each
\texttt{cmp}'d frame-by-frame against the single-process CPU reference.

\paragraph{Benchmark (\texttt{bench\_dynamic.sh}).} Four configurations:
the laptop-alone baseline (re-run in-batch --- absolute walls drift between
batches, \S\ref{sec:noise}); \texttt{weighted\_static} 5:1 (one invocation
per node via \texttt{dist\_frames.sh WEIGHTS=5,1}); \texttt{dyn\_51}
(stealing coordinator, calibrated weights); and \texttt{dyn\_11} (stealing
coordinator, deliberately \emph{wrong} equal weights --- the robustness
probe: how much of equal-share static's $+150\%$ does stealing recover with
no correct information?). The benchmark ran twice: once before the chunk
cap (\texttt{results\_dynamic\_uncapped.csv}) and once with it
(\texttt{results\_dynamic.csv}, the shipped configuration); the first run
is reported because it measured the defect the cap fixes. 5:1 approximates
the measured $4.56\times$ throughput ratio (the optimal 82/18 split vs.\
5:1's 83/17 --- indistinguishable at $F=100$).

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Calibrated weights reach baseline parity --- the ceiling realized, no more}
\label{sec:parity}

Weighted static lands at $22.93\pm0.11$~s and dynamic 5:1 at
$22.01\pm0.56$~s against a $21.06\pm2.73$~s in-batch baseline --- $0.7\sigma$
and $0.3\sigma$ of the baseline's own spread: statistically
indistinguishable from not distributing at all. This is exactly what
report~25's arithmetic said the pair allows: the ideal weighted wall is
$15.4$~s, the measured orchestration overhead is $3.1$--$3.4$~s here (e2e
minus the slower node's compute wall: $23.0-19.9$ static,
$22.0-19.0$ dynamic), and the residual is the weighted-random draw's
variance (\S\ref{sec:variance}). The meaningful comparison is against
equal shares: $46.90 \to 22$--$23$~s, a $2.1\times$ improvement from
weighting alone. The conclusion of report~25 stands with its constructive
half now measured: a $4.56\times$-slower node cannot shorten this wall, but
weighted shares stop it from \emph{lengthening} it --- ivy now contributes
its 15--17 frames for free instead of costing $+150\%$.

\subsection{The unstealable in-flight chunk, and the weight-proportional cap}
\label{sec:cap}

The uncapped \texttt{dyn\_11} run is the diagnostic. Its chunk trace shows
the defect in one line each: ivy's first guided chunk was half its 50-frame
bag --- \textbf{25 frames = 27.4~s in flight} --- while the laptop finished
its own 50 \emph{plus everything it could steal} (25 frames, in chunks of
13/6/6) in $16.4$~s of busy time and then idled $\sim$11~s. Stealing moves
only \emph{undispatched} frames; a chunk already handed to a node is
invisible to the bags, so the slow node's oversized first chunk bound the
wall at $31.27\pm1.08$~s regardless of how well the laptop stole.

The fix (commit \texttt{8c21bf4}) prices exposure in time rather than
frames: an in-flight chunk of $k$ frames occupies its node for
$k/\mathrm{rate}_r$, and rate is proportional to weight, so capping every
chunk at $\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ frames ($\mathrm{KCAP}=8$) bounds every
node's unstealable exposure at the \emph{same} wall-time budget ---
$\sim$7~s on both ivy ($8\times0.855$) and the laptop
($40\times0.188$). Measured effect on \texttt{dyn\_11}: ivy's busy time
$28.0 \to 22.2$~s, the laptop's stolen haul $25 \to 29$ frames, e2e
$31.27 \to 28.18\pm0.37$~s ($-9.9\%$, $2.7\sigma$). The fast node keeps its
large chunks (few startups on the critical path); only the node whose
in-flight work can bind gets fine-grained.

\subsection{Stealing recovers 72\% of a miscalibration autonomously}
\label{sec:recovery}

With no correct speed information at all (1:1 weights), the capped stealer
runs $28.18$~s where equal-share static ran $46.90$~s --- $72\%$ of the
$25.8$~s penalty recovered with zero configuration ($67\%$ against the
more favourable uncapped-batch baseline; the claim survives the drift).
The mechanism: the laptop empties its 50-frame bag in $\sim$9~s, then
strips ivy's bag from the tail in weight-capped chunks, ending at 79/21 ---
close to the 82/18 optimum the weights failed to encode. The residual
$+34\%$ over baseline decomposes into ivy's still-inflated share (21 frames
$\times$ deep-draw cost $= 22.2$~s busy vs.\ $16.3$~s at 5:1) and
$6.0$~s of per-chunk dispatch overhead on the binding node (e2e $-$ ivy
busy; $\sim$3 chunk boundaries $\times$ SSH+scp+startup) --- the price of
the fine granularity that made the rescue possible. Stealing is a recovery
mechanism, not a substitute for calibration: it converges toward the right
split at chunk granularity, paying dispatch overhead for every correction
step.

\subsection{With calibrated weights, stealing never fires --- and costs nothing}
\label{sec:insurance}

In all three \texttt{dyn\_51} reps the steal count is zero: the 83/17 deal
finishes within $\sim$2.7~s on both nodes (laptop busy $18.96$, ivy
$16.31$~s), so no bag empties while another still holds frames. Dynamic
mode at correct weights therefore measures pure chunking overhead vs.\
one-shot weighted static: $22.01$ vs.\ $22.93$~s --- a wash inside noise
(the dynamic mean is actually \emph{lower}; its guided first chunks also
start the laptop's largest work earlier). The implication: there is no
premium for running the stealer as the default --- it is free insurance
that activates only when the weights are wrong
(\S\ref{sec:recovery}) or the hardware changes out from under them
(\S\ref{sec:corrective}).

\subsection{The live corrective steal: weights are configuration-dependent}
\label{sec:corrective}

The production identity check ran both nodes CPU-pinned --- and there the
laptop is only $\sim$$2.4\times$ ivy ($0.35$ vs.\ $0.855$~s/frame), not
$4.56\times$: the 5:1 weights that are correct for the hybrid
\emph{overload} a CPU-pinned laptop. The stealer corrected it unprompted:
ivy finished its 17 frames and stole 5 back from the laptop's tail
(\texttt{[dyn] rank 1 chunk 3 (steal0): 5 frames in 6.18s}), and the run
stayed 100/100 byte-identical. This is the cleanest demonstration of what
the dynamic layer is \emph{for}: the right weights are not a property of
the machines but of the (machine, executor-mix) pair --- the same laptop is
worth 5 ivys with its GPU and 2.4 without --- and stealing absorbs exactly
that model error, in either direction.

\subsection{Two noise sources: the fixed random draw, and baseline drift}
\label{sec:variance}
\label{sec:noise}

Two honest caveats. First, the weighted-random draw has real variance at
small shares: ivy's seed-1234 15-frame share costs $19.9$~s $= 1.32$~s/frame
--- $55\%$ above its $0.855$ population mean, because 15 draws from a
$32\times$-spread cost distribution can land deep-heavy, and the fixed seed
makes this \emph{bias, not noise}, across reps ($\sigma=0.11$~s on a
mis-centred mean). A deterministic weighted interleave would cut the
variance; with stealing on top it is moot (the dynamic tail absorbs it).
Second, the laptop baseline drifted across this day's batches
($18.75\pm0.34 \to 19.20\pm0.55 \to 21.06\pm2.73$~s, one $24.1$~s rep ---
sustained benchmarking thermals), which is why every $\Delta$ in
Table~\ref{tab:bench} is computed against its own batch's baseline and why
parity, not a signed few-percent delta, is the defensible claim.

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Default heterogeneous runs to
        \texttt{dist\_dynamic.sh} with measured weights.} Calibrate from
        solo walls (here $85.48/18.75 \approx 4.6 \Rightarrow$
        \texttt{WEIGHTS=5,1}); expect parity with the fast node alone
        ($22.0$ vs.\ $21.1$~s) and $2.1\times$ over equal shares. The
        stealing layer costs nothing when the weights are right
        (0 steals, $-0.9$~s vs.\ static, within noise).
  \item \textbf{Keep the weight-proportional chunk cap
        ($\mathrm{KCAP}=8$).} It is worth $-9.9\%$ ($2.7\sigma$) on the
        miscalibrated case by bounding unstealable exposure at
        $\sim$7~s/node. Trade-off: more chunk boundaries on the binding
        node ($6.0$~s dispatch overhead in \texttt{dyn\_11}); lowering
        KCAP below 8 buys finer rescue granularity only if per-chunk
        overhead ($\sim$1.5--2~s: SSH, scp, process start) comes down
        first.
  \item \textbf{Do not encode weights per machine; encode them per
        (machine, executor-mix).} The same pair is 4.56:1 hybrid and
        2.4:1 CPU-pinned (\S\ref{sec:corrective}); a hosts file that
        changes \texttt{gpuEnable} must change weights too --- or lean on
        the stealer, which corrected the 5:1/CPU-pinned mismatch by
        exactly 5 frames on its own.
  \item \textbf{For static-only runs at small shares, consider a
        deterministic weighted interleave.} The seeded random draw put
        $+55\%$ on ivy's 15-frame share as a fixed bias
        (\S\ref{sec:variance}). Not worth building while the stealer
        exists; worth remembering if \texttt{DIST\_WEIGHTS} is ever used
        bare on a slow node.
  \item \textbf{Report-25 conclusions stand.} Parity is the ceiling for
        this pair; a slow node buys capacity, not latency. The now-open
        measurement is the same weighted/dynamic A/B on comparable nodes,
        where the ideal is $\sim$$2\times$ rather than $1.22\times$ and
        the interleave study (report 13's earmark) becomes readable.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The two schedulers report~25's arithmetic called for are built, validated,
and measured. The seeded weighted-random assignment gives every node a
speed-proportional random share with zero communication and keeps the
distribution 100/100 byte-identical at production scale; the coordinator
stealer adds per-chunk dispatch through the new \texttt{DIST\_FRAMES}
interface and moves undispatched tails to whichever node runs dry. With
calibrated 5:1 weights, both modes sit at baseline parity
($22.9/22.0$ vs.\ $21.1\pm2.7$~s) --- the report-25 ceiling realized, and
$2.1\times$ better than equal shares. With wrong weights, stealing recovers
$72\%$ of the penalty on its own, but only once chunks are capped at
$\mathrm{KCAP}\cdot w_r$ frames --- the uncapped run measured why: a slow
node's 25-frame half-bag chunk sat unstealable for $27.4$~s and bound the
wall. And the CPU-pinned identity run showed the stealer doing its real
job unasked: correcting weights that were right for one executor mix and
wrong for another, five frames at a time, byte-identically.
