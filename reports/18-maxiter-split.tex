\begin{abstract}
The subdivision heuristic computes a region (rather than splitting it) when its
nine stencil samples are ``uniform'': $\texttt{maxIter}-\texttt{minIter} <
\texttt{diffThresh}\cdot\texttt{maxIter}$. Inspecting the visualisations suggests
that at the tuned $\texttt{diffThresh}=0.1$ this is, in practice, just ``don't
split if the region is interior'' --- the cheapest way for nine samples to be
similar is for all of them to run to \texttt{MAXITER}. This report tests that by
replacing the relative-spread test with a parameter-free binary rule: compute iff
all nine samples reached \texttt{MAXITER} (\texttt{minIter}$=$frame
\texttt{MAXITER}), else split. The result is decisive: the two rules produce
\textbf{byte-identical} decompositions on the production zoom (\textbf{5608}
leaves both) and on three of the four report-13 regimes. They diverge only on
\textbf{outside} (uniform deep exterior): there \texttt{diffThreshold} keeps a
large uniform-exterior region as one leaf, while the binary rule splits it to the
floor ($+22\,\%$ leaves) --- at \emph{zero} wall cost, since those leaves are
trivial iter-2 exterior. So $\texttt{diffThreshold}=0.1$ \emph{is} a parameter-free
interior certificate on realistic content; the threshold's only distinctive job
is to avoid shattering large uniform-exterior regions, which is cost-free here.
This explains why $0.1$ is the sweet spot.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Setup}

\paragraph{The decision under test.} \texttt{MandelRegion::examine} computes a
region or splits it into four by one line:

\begin{sourcecode}{cpp}{mandelregion.cpp -- the split decision (current vs the experiment).}
// current (diffThreshold relative-spread test):
if (maxIter - minIter < diffThresh * maxIter || pixelsX * pixelsY < pixelSizeThresh)
// experiment (binary "all 9 samples in-set"):
if (minIter == ownerFrame->MAXITER       || pixelsX * pixelsY < pixelSizeThresh)
\end{sourcecode}

The hypothesis (from reading the \texttt{viz} overlays, reports 08/13): at
$\texttt{diffThresh}=0.1$ the relative test is so tight that exterior regions
almost always split, and the only regions uniform enough to be computed are the
interior ones, where all nine samples reach \texttt{MAXITER}. If so, the
parameter-free rule on the right --- compute iff every sample is in-set --- should
reproduce the same decomposition. The pixel floor (\texttt{pixelSizeThresh}) is
unchanged, so boundary regions still terminate; \texttt{diffThresh} becomes unused.

\paragraph{Method.} Leaf counts are deterministic --- the decomposition is
thread-independent (verified byte-identical across executors in report~08) --- so
a single GPU-only run (\texttt{numThreads}=1) gives the exact leaf count per spec.
Compared on the production \texttt{spec.in} (the canonical minibrot zoom) and the
four report-13 zoom-point specs (\texttt{experiments/17-ncu-zoom-points/specs/}),
all at $\texttt{diffT}=0.1$, $\texttt{pixT}=32768$. Wall time is measured GPU-only
on the one spec where the rules differ.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{On realistic content, \texttt{diffThreshold}=0.1 \emph{is} the
interior certificate}

Table~\ref{tab:leaves}, Figure~\ref{fig:leaves}: the binary all-MAXITER rule
reproduces the $\texttt{diffT}=0.1$ decomposition \textbf{exactly} on the
production zoom (5608 leaves either way) and on inside, Misiurewicz, and seahorse.
The hypothesis is confirmed: on this content the two rules are the same function.

The mechanism is that the relative-spread test \emph{collapses} to the
\texttt{MAXITER} test at $\texttt{diffThresh}=0.1$. A region is computed only if
$\texttt{maxIter}-\texttt{minIter} < 0.1\cdot\texttt{maxIter}$, i.e.\ all nine
samples lie within $10\,\%$ of the largest. On boundary or interior content, the
escape iteration varies sharply across the fractal boundary, so any region that
is not pure interior has spread well above $10\,\%$ and splits; the only regions
tight enough to compute are those where all nine samples reach \texttt{MAXITER}
(spread exactly zero). The two predicates therefore select the same regions. This
is why the canonical minibrot zoom --- boundary- and interior-dominated --- is
byte-identical.

\subsection{The one divergence: large uniform exterior}

The rules differ only on \texttt{outside}: a zoom into $c=(1,1)$, far from the
set, where every pixel escapes at iteration~2. A large region there has all nine
samples equal to 2 --- spread zero, but \emph{not} \texttt{MAXITER}. The
relative test computes it as one big leaf; the binary rule, seeing samples that
are not in-set, splits it to the pixel floor ($+264$ leaves, $+22\,\%$).

This is the one thing \texttt{diffThreshold}'s relative formulation buys over a
binary interior test: it recognises \emph{any} uniform region, interior or
exterior, and computes it whole. The implication, though, is that the difference
is free: Table~\ref{tab:leaves} shows the \texttt{outside} wall is flat
($\sim$5.8~s under both), because the extra leaves are trivial iter-2 exterior ---
splitting them adds kernel launches but essentially no compute (work is
conserved; reports 09/16). So the binary rule is not worse here in any way that
costs time; it is only \emph{less elegant}, shattering regions a relative test
would keep whole.

\subsection{Why \texttt{diffThreshold}=0.1 is the sweet spot}

This reframes the $\texttt{diffThreshold}$ tuning of reports 06/07/10. The reason
$0.1$ is optimal is that it is the threshold at which the rule becomes a clean
interior certificate on boundary content: tight enough that only the genuinely
in-set regions compute, loose enough not to fight the pixel floor. A larger
$\texttt{diffThreshold}$ (0.5) admits near-uniform but non-interior regions as
leaves --- computing regions that should have split --- which is exactly the
$+1$--$2\,\%$ wall penalty reports 05/06 measured at 0.5. A smaller one (0.05)
saturates against \texttt{pixelSizeThresh} and stops changing the decomposition.
So $0.1$ is not a delicate optimum; it is the operating point where the heuristic
coincides with ``compute the interior, split everything else,'' which the leaf
counts here show is the decomposition the workload wants.

\section{Implications}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The heuristic could be made parameter-free with no loss on this
        workload.} Replacing the \texttt{diffThreshold} test with the binary
        all-MAXITER rule removes a tuning knob and reproduces the production
        decomposition exactly (5608 leaves). It is a legitimate simplification
        for Mandelbrot zooms dominated by boundary and interior content.
  \item \textbf{But keep \texttt{diffThreshold} for robustness.} The binary rule
        degrades on uniform-\emph{exterior} content (\texttt{outside}: $+22\,\%$
        leaves), shattering large coherent regions the relative test computes
        whole. The penalty is cost-free \emph{here} (trivial exterior), but on a
        workload with expensive uniform-exterior regions it would add real launch
        and bookkeeping overhead. The relative test is the safer general rule;
        this experiment simply shows it is doing nothing extra on the canonical
        zoom.
  \item \textbf{This is not a performance lever.} On the production workload the
        decompositions are identical, so wall time is identical. The value is
        explanatory: it pins down what $\texttt{diffThreshold}=0.1$ actually does
        and why it is optimal --- not a new optimisation. The branch is kept off
        \texttt{main} as a characterization (like the priority-queue branch,
        report~11).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

Replacing the \texttt{diffThreshold} relative-spread test with a parameter-free
``compute iff all nine samples reached \texttt{MAXITER}'' rule reproduces the
$\texttt{diffT}=0.1$ decomposition \textbf{byte-identically} on the production
zoom (5608 leaves) and on inside, Misiurewicz, and seahorse. The two rules diverge
only on uniform deep exterior (\texttt{outside}, $+22\,\%$ leaves), at zero wall
cost. So on realistic Mandelbrot content $\texttt{diffThreshold}=0.1$ is, exactly,
a parameter-free interior certificate; the threshold's relative formulation only
earns its keep on large uniform-exterior regions, which is cost-free here. This
explains why $0.1$ is the tuned optimum --- it is the operating point where the
heuristic coincides with ``compute the interior, split everything else.''
