\begin{abstract}
Reports 16/17 pinned this kernel at exactly 100.0\% warp execution
efficiency on interior content, and report 21 cited that number to defer
the GPU-side periodicity check (``expect a loss''). Report 23 falsified the
conclusion at the wall ($-20.5\%$ hybrid); this report replaces the stale
counters. Same method as experiment 16 (same four content-regime specs,
same kernel caps, same metrics; aggregation validated to reproduce
experiment 16's summary exactly; clocks match within 1\%, so durations
compare). The price of the early exit is small and precisely locatable:
interior warp efficiency falls only $\mathbf{100.0 \to 96.3\%}$ (minimum
kernel 95.0\%) while interior kernels get $\mathbf{9.9\times}$ faster;
the isolated overhead --- pure-exterior content, where the check never
fires --- is $\mathbf{+12.4\%}$, half the 25--30\% estimate, because
\texttt{nvcc} short-circuits the two anchor compares into one
always-executed \texttt{DSETP}. The cost concentrates on boundary content
($+23\%$, kernels production routes to the CPU anyway); the production mix
nets $9.4\times$ per kernel. The FP64 pipe stays at $\sim$85\% of peak in
every regime, before and after: the kernel remains FP64-bound, and every
metric that ``degraded'' --- warp efficiency, achieved occupancy --- reads
worse precisely because the work now ends early.
\end{abstract}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Motivation: replace the number the wrong prediction leaned on}
\label{sec:motivation}

Report 16 measured interior kernels at exactly 100.0\% warp execution
efficiency and 85.6\% of FP64 peak; report 17 reproduced both on the four
zoom-point geometries. Those were correct measurements read as health:
report 21's recommendation 6 cited them to predict the GPU-side
periodicity check would lose. Report 23 measured the check instead ---
mix-controlled kernel $2.34\times$, hybrid $29.83 \to 23.72$~s, output
byte-identical --- and identified the misreading: on interior content,
``100\% efficiency, zero early exits'' \emph{is} the waste (every lane of
every warp grinding provably-useless iterations to \texttt{MAXITER} in
perfect lockstep). What report 23 could not say is what the counters look
like now: how much warp efficiency the early exit actually spends, where
the two-compares-per-iteration overhead lands, and whether the FP64
roofline moved. Without a measured replacement, the 100.0\% baseline
stays on record as a target someone could ``restore'' by deleting the
check. This experiment re-baselines all of it.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Identical to experiment 16, on the merged v7 binary: the same four
content-regime specs reused verbatim (\emph{interior} = main cardioid,
$\texttt{maxIter}$ 2000--3000; \emph{exterior} = fast escape;
\emph{boundary} = seahorse valley; \emph{canonical} = the production
zoom), GPU-only runs (\texttt{1 1 0.1 32768 1 0} --- the single GPU thread
sees every region class), \texttt{--set full} on 6/6/60/60 kernels plus a
cheap warp/branch \texttt{--metrics} pass on 6/6/300/300. Counters are
admin-gated; capture ran via \texttt{sudo capture.sh}, analysis
unprivileged via \texttt{analyze.py}.

\textbf{Baseline.} Experiment 16's tracked \texttt{analysis/summary.csv}.
The device \texttt{diverge()} is unchanged between \texttt{binary-v5-affinity}
(which experiment 16 captured) and v6 (a CPU-only change), so no
re-capture of the old kernel is needed. \texttt{analyze.py} reproduces
experiment 16's aggregation exactly --- validated in-session against its
tracked summary on all four regimes (simple mean over captured kernels of
the same raw-page columns). One slip found in the reference: experiment
16's \emph{exterior} \texttt{dur\_ms} is recorded in \textmu s (614.869);
corrected to 0.615~ms here.

\textbf{Comparability.} Warp/branch efficiency, pipe and SOL percentages,
and occupancy are clock-independent ratios. Durations normally carry a
thermal caveat across sessions, but the recorded GPC clocks match within
1\% (v6: 1.317--1.345~GHz; v7: 1.322--1.342~GHz), so the duration ratios
are quoted as measured. The decomposition is deterministic and identical
in both binaries (report 23), so capture $N$ sees the same $N$ regions.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Interior pays 3.7 points because detection is synchronized
within a warp}

The feared divergence catastrophe is a 3.7-point drop: interior kernels
average 96.3\% warp efficiency (minimum 95.0\%, nothing below 90\% in any
capture) while running $9.9\times$ faster. The reason detection is so
coherent: a warp's 32 lanes are contiguous pixels of the same interior
region converging to the same attracting cycle, and every lane refreshes
its Brent anchor at the \emph{same} iteration numbers (8, 16, 32, \ldots\
--- the schedule is iteration-indexed, not state-indexed). Neighbouring
orbits therefore satisfy the exact-revisit test at nearly the same
iteration, and the masked-idle tail between the first and last detection
in a warp is short relative to even the shortened kernel. The duration
ratio quantifies the effective latency: $9.9\times$ at
\texttt{maxIter} 2000--3000 and $+12.4\%$ per-iteration overhead implies
the slowest lane detects at $\sim$225 iterations on main-cardioid content
--- between report 21's 67 (period-3 bulb) and $\sim$900 (deep minibrot),
as expected for the period-1 cardioid interior with a slow transient.

\subsection{The isolated overhead is $+12.4\%$, not 25--30\%: \texttt{nvcc}
short-circuits the compare pair}

Exterior content prices the check's overhead with zero benefit mixed in:
no escaping orbit ever revisits a state, so the branch never fires, and
the regime stays at exactly 100.0\% warp efficiency --- the $+12.4\%$
duration is pure per-iteration cost. Reports 21/23 estimated 25--30\% by
counting two FP64 compares against a $\sim$7-instruction recurrence; the
SASS shows why the measured cost is half that: the compiler compiles
\texttt{vx == sx \&\& vy == sy} as one always-executed
\texttt{DSETP.NEU} on \texttt{vx}, with the \texttt{vy} compare
(\texttt{DSETP.EQ}) predicated behind it --- it almost never issues on
non-cycling orbits. One extra FP64-pipe instruction on a loop body of
$\sim$8 predicts $+12.5\%$; measured, $+12.4\%$. Consistently, the FP64
pipe stays at $\sim$85\% of peak in every regime: the check did not move
the roofline, it added one slot to the iteration.

\subsection{Boundary pays the most ($+23\%$) and produces nothing --- and
production doesn't care}

Boundary kernels slow $16.8 \to 20.7$~ms with a warp-efficiency
distribution that is statistically identical (mean $70.7 \to 70.2$, 47\%
below 70\% in both, minimum $\sim$28.5\%): seahorse-valley content
iterates long but either escapes or fails to \emph{exactly} cycle within
the anchor schedule, so it pays the compare on every iteration and the
early exit almost never fires. The $+23\%$ exceeding the $+12.5\%$
static floor is the one number these counters do not fully attribute
(candidates: the predicated second compare firing on near-cyclic orbits,
and thinner warps hiding the recurrence latency less well); it is also the
least consequential --- report 16 showed these are the $240\times135$
pixel-floor leaves, and in the production hybrid the affinity queue routes
them to the CPU pool. The production mix (canonical) nets $9.4\times$ per
kernel regardless.

\subsection{The occupancy drop is the early exit's signature, not register
pressure}

Registers rise $22 \to 28$ (the two anchor doubles and the schedule
counter live across the loop), which moves the register occupancy limit
from 10 to 8 blocks/SM --- both far above the 4-block warp limit that
actually binds ($4 \times 256 = 1024$ threads/SM), so \emph{theoretical}
occupancy is unchanged. The achieved drop ($98.9 \to 89.5\%$ on interior)
is the same phenomenon as the warp-efficiency drop seen one level up:
warps retire as their lanes detect cycles, blocks drain, and the SM
spends a larger fraction of a much shorter kernel partially full. Like
warp efficiency, achieved occupancy now reads ``worse'' \emph{because}
the kernel stops doing useless work.

\subsection{The counters reconcile with report 23's $2.34\times$, and the
metrics' role changes}

Report 23 measured the mix-controlled kernel total at $2.34\times$ over
all 5{,}608 regions; these captures show interior-type content at
$\sim$$9.9\times$ and never-cycling content at $\sim$$1.2\times$ slower.
The blend $s/9.9 + (1-s)\times1.2 = 1/2.34$ solves to $s \approx 0.70$:
seventy percent of the v6 kernel time was interior grind --- the
GPU-side echo of the leaf-purity census that motivated report 21. The
practical conclusion for the metric suite: on this kernel, warp execution
efficiency and achieved occupancy are no longer health indicators; they
are the accounting of eliminated work. The FP64 pipe percentage
($\sim$85\%, invariant) remains the real health line --- it says the
kernel is still FP64-bound, so the next kernel-side lever (if one is ever
needed) is still arithmetic, not scheduling.

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Adopt these numbers as the healthy baselines}: interior
        96.3\% warp / 99.7\% branch efficiency, canonical distribution
        mean 78.1\% with 32\% of kernels below 70\%, achieved occupancy
        89.5\%, FP64 pipe 85\%. Reports 16/17's 100.0\% figures are
        superseded by design; a future capture that reads 100.0\% on
        interior content means the check was removed and $\sim$$10\times$
        kernel time came back.
  \item \textbf{Do not pursue $k$-strided compare amortization.} The
        motivation (reports 21/23's 25--30\% overhead estimate) is gone:
        the short-circuited compare costs $+12.4\%$, and striding the
        anchor compare to every $k$-th iteration would delay detection by
        up to $k-1$ iterations on a $\sim$225-iteration prize to recover
        part of one instruction in eight. The measured ceiling is not
        worth the exactness-argument complication.
  \item \textbf{Proceed to the \texttt{scanLine} commit path} (report 23,
        recommendation 2). The kernel side is done: $9.4\times$ on the
        production mix with the pipe still 85\% saturated leaves the GPU
        thread's host side (12.8~s vs 10.7~s kernel at steady state) as
        the only lane cost that has not been attacked.
  \item \textbf{Port the check to \texttt{oclkernel.cpp} before the third
        iGPU repricing} (report 23, recommendation 4) --- unchanged, with
        one addition from this data: expect the iGPU's kernel-side gain to
        be content-identical ($\sim$$10\times$ on interior) but its warp
        (subgroup) metrics to shift the same way; budget the same
        identity-gate-against-itself validation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The GPU-side periodicity check's microarchitectural price is measured and
small: interior warp execution efficiency $100.0 \to 96.3\%$ (minimum
95.0\%), achieved occupancy $98.9 \to 89.5\%$, six extra registers with
the theoretical occupancy limit untouched, and $+12.4\%$ per-iteration
overhead where no cycle exists --- half the pre-measurement estimate,
because \texttt{nvcc} short-circuits the anchor compares. The prize the
metrics paid for: interior kernels $9.9\times$, the production mix
$9.4\times$ per kernel, reconciling to report 23's mix-controlled
$2.34\times$ via a 70/30 interior/other time split. Boundary floor leaves
pay $+23\%$ and remain exactly as divergent as report 16 found them ---
structural, size-bound, and routed to the CPU in production. The FP64
pipe stays at $\sim$85\% of peak in every regime: the kernel is still
FP64-bound, the roofline did not move, and the metrics that ``got worse''
are the ledger of the work that no longer happens. Reports 16/17's
100.0\% baselines are hereby replaced; the queued levers are unchanged:
\texttt{scanLine} host-side commit, then the iGPU port and repricing.
